takeda sales analytics

an experience design case study

setting the scene

In 2010 the Takeda Oncology marketing team instituted a digital-first sales initiative. They created a cross-funtional group to spearhead operations which would hopefully encourage sales force utilization of the new digital materials over traditional print marketing tools. The team believed that the more exposure the field users had to using their iPads, the higher their comfort level would be, thereby decreasing digital inhibition. Going forward any communication, reporting or resources the field previously received in a PDF or print format would be exclusively available from a mobile device.

Unfortunately the users were quickly innundated with an abundance of apps all using different, and often uneccesarily complicated, interfaces. Instead of driving digital usage these apps became one of the key barriers to user adoption of future digital offerings.

In 2012, the Commercial and Business Systems group started investing resources in a dedicated user experience team. An essential task for this team was rebuilding relationships throughout the business and customer groups. This subsequently led to better communication, collaboration and access to customers. The team spent time interviewing end-users about their needs and use cases then proposed changes to preexisting apps in order to improve user retention and satisfaction. In addition to app specific modifications, they helped to identify roadblocks to digital usage from a wholistic standpoint which led to the creation of interaction and usability standards. The UX team quickly became an integral asset across the organization.

overview of the project

With the help of the UX team, digital adoption grew slightly; however, one particular app remained a heavy roadblock to full adoption. In 2015, the experience team proposed a complete redesign of the sales analytics tool. Instead of using an out-of-the-box solution, the new tool would be designed from the ground up to accomodate the organization's unique reporting requirements while also balancing the user's needs.

our experience design philosophy

The key component to a successful UX practice is the ability to remain an impartial advocate for the best possible customer experience. At the end of the day, we are neither the business owners or end-users of the systems we design. Our role requires us to truly understand what the customers need regardless of our own opinions or predispositions. We have to balance complex business requirements with technical capabilities and often play the role of mediator with a wide array of project teams.

research + requirement gathering

In the excitement of launching a new initiative, key stakeholders regularly push to get the design and development phases of a project going as soon as possible. Unfortunately once a project moves into the development phase, fixing issues costs far more than it would have if identified during the design phase. The costs increase exponentially if issues are not identified until after the project is released to the end-users.

Before drafting a single content map, wireframe or requirements doc the UX team dedicated time to interviewing the project stakeholders and as many end-users as possible. This research served as the backbone for subsequent design conversations throughout the project.

design, development + user engagement

After reviewing the business requirements and customer use cases, the UX team developed a series proposed content structures which were then presented to the stakeholders. The agreed upon content map became the basis for wireframes, prototypes and mock-ups. The business stakeholders remained highly involved through the design process, while the end-users were brought in to give feedback on interactive prototypes and mock-ups. Using this iterative and inclusive approach we were able to address a large number of design concerns prior to development.

In addition the UX team regularly met with the technical groups for design reviews. These constant touch points helped the developers understand the business drivers behind the designs and also provided them with opportunities to share suggestions or express concerns regarding platform limitations.

Once the designs received sign-off from the business the project moved into the development phase. As with any software endeavor the solution continued to evolve as the dev team came across potential improvements as well as performance and functionality challenges. The UX team continued to provided guidance and creative options that varied from the approved designs but still maintained the integrity of the user experience.

Towards the final stages of development key stakeholders and end-users participated in product testing. At this stage some minor updates were identified, addressed and retested prior to the final app build.

customer feedback

Prior to launch, the end-users participated in mandatory training workshops which covered the new reporting interface as well as important considerations when viewing the data. The customer feedback during the workshop and in our post launch surveys was uniformly positive. Additionally, the UX team scheduled monthly touch-point calls open to any end-users who had questions or wanted to offer suggestions for future releases. Overall, the project is considered to be the most successful mobile initiative to date.

ongoing analytics

In addition to user research, we developed custom analytics to track usage trends. Less than four weeks after rolling out the new app our mobile reporting rate jumped from less than 15% (highest rate achieved in the last 6 months using the legacy reporting tool) to over 85% of users accessing their data on a weekly basis. The data also helped us to identify the least used sections of the app. These areas have subsequently been revamped or repurposed in subsequent releases.
